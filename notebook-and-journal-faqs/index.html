Notebook And Journal questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Notebook and journal buyers often wonder about paper quality, binding durability, and page ruling. The best choice depends on your writing tool (fountain pens, ballpoints, pencils) and whether you plan to carry it daily or keep it on a desk. Look for paper weight over 90 gsm for minimal bleed-through, and choose sewn binding for lay-flat pages.

What type of paper is best for fountain pens?

Fountain pen users need paper that resists feathering and bleeding. Look for paper described as “fountain pen friendly” or with a weight of 90 gsm or higher; these papers are typically coated or made from finer fibers. Many owners of popular notebooks report that Tomoe River and similar papers handle wet inks beautifully. Avoid standard copy paper or very thin office notebooks, as they will show heavy ghosting and may bleed through. If you use extra-wet nibs or shimmer inks, you may need paper that is even heavier, around 120 gsm, but that can mean a bulkier notebook.

What are the different binding styles and their pros and cons?

The most common bindings are sewn (or Smyth-sewn), staple-bound, spiral-bound, and glued. Sewn binding allows the notebook to lie completely flat when open, which is ideal for writing across the gutter. It is also very durable and resists pages falling out over time. Staple-bound notebooks are thin and affordable but cannot lay flat. Spiral-bound notebooks open easily and can fold back, but the coils can snag on bags. Glued (perfect-bound) notebooks are smooth on the spine but may eventually crack or lose pages if forced open too wide. For long-term use, sewn binding is widely recommended by frequent journalers.

How do I choose the right paper size for my needs?

Size matters for portability and writing comfort. A5 is the most versatile size: large enough for substantial journal entries but small enough to slip into many bags. Pocket-sized (A6 or similar) is perfect for on-the-go jotting or a wallet notebook. Larger sizes like B5 or A4 are better for desk use, brainstorming, or if you need a lot of space per page. Keep in mind that oversized notebooks can be heavy to carry and may not fit in standard bags. Many buyers start with A5 and adjust from there based on how they actually use the notebook.

What page ruling or layout should I pick?

The most popular layouts are lined, dotted, blank, and squared. Lined paper is classic for linear journaling and note-taking; it keeps handwriting straight. Dotted paper (dot grid) is the favorite of bullet journalists because it provides guide points without the visual clutter of lines. Blank pages are best for artists or those who want total freedom for sketches and diagrams, but they can make neat handwriting harder. Squared (graph) paper is excellent for technical drawings, tables, or spacing letters evenly. If you are unsure, dotted is a safe middle ground that suits many uses.

Hard cover vs. soft cover: which is more durable?

Hard covers offer superior protection for the pages when tossed in a bag or used on uneven surfaces. They also provide a firm writing surface when you are not at a desk. However, hardcovers add weight and bulk, and the metal corners or spines can sometimes snag. Soft covers are lighter, more flexible, and often easier to slip into a pocket or crowded backpack. They are less forgiving if the notebook gets bent or crushed. For everyday carry enthusiasts, a quality soft cover with a thick card stock or leatherette finish is a popular compromise that balances durability with portability.

Frequently asked questions

Is lined or dotted better for bullet journaling?

Dotted is widely considered the best for bullet journaling because the guides help keep boxes and lines straight without overwhelming the page. Lined works too, but the lines can interfere with drawings and layouts.

Why do some notebooks have numbered pages?

Numbered pages are a huge convenience for creating a custom index or table of contents, especially in bullet journals or study notebooks. They save you the time of numbering each page by hand.

What does “ghosting” mean in paper terms?

Ghosting is the faint shadow of writing or drawing visible on the other side of the page. It is common with thinner papers and does not always mean bleed-through. Some ghosting is normal, but heavy ghosting can make both sides harder to read.

Can I use markers in a standard notebook?

Most standard notebook papers will bleed through with alcohol markers or heavy brush pens. If markers are your main tool, look for paper that is explicitly marketed as marker-safe, typically with a weight of 150 gsm or higher, or ask experienced reviewers about bleed performance.

Are recycled paper notebooks as good?

Not all recycled papers are equal. Many lower-cost recycled notebooks use shorter fibers that can cause feathering with fountain pens. Higher-end recycled papers blended with cotton or hemp can perform well, but you will need to check owner reviews for compatibility with your writing tools.

How important is the cover material for daily use?

Cover material affects both durability and feel. Cloth covers can stain easily, while leatherette or polyurethane covers are easy to wipe clean. If you carry your notebook everywhere, a coated or water-resistant cover is wise to protect the pages inside.

In shortChoosing the right notebook or journal comes down to matching paper performance, binding, size, and layout to how you actually write and where you take it. Read owner reviews for the specific combination you have in mind, and do not be afraid to try a few different options before settling on a favorite.
